Practice Problems Solution


  • What must be the distance between point charge q1 = 52 Î¼C and point charge q2 = -48 Î¼C for the electrostatic force between them to have a magnitude of 7.70 N?

    • Solution: Given data:
    q1 = 52 Î¼C = 52*10-6C;
    q2 = -48 Î¼C = -48*10-6C
    F=7.70N
    We know that, K = 1010
    r=?



    Coulomb's law equation: \(F = \frac{K \cdot q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2}\)
    Equation for \(r^2 = \frac{K \cdot q_1 \cdot q_2}{F}\)
    Equation for \(r = \sqrt{\frac{K \cdot q_1 \cdot q_2}{F}}\)
    \(r = \sqrt{\frac{{10^{10} \cdot (52 \times 10^{-6}) \cdot (48 \times 10^{-6})}}{{7.70}}}\)
    r =1.8 m

  • A particle of charge +3.20* 10-6C is 12.0 cm distance from a second particle of charge -1.6*10-6C. Calculate the magnitude of the electrostatic force between the particles.


    • Solution: Given data:
    q1 = +3.20* 10-6C
    q2 = -1.6*10-6C
    r = 12cm = 0.12m
    We know that, K = 1010
    F = ?



    Coulomb's law equation: \(F = \frac{K \cdot q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2}\)
    \( F = \frac{10^{10} \times 3.20 \times 10^{-6} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-6}}{{(0.12)^2}} \)
    F = 3.6 N

  • Two equally charged particles are held 5.20Ã—10-3 m apart and then released from rest. The initial acceleration of the first particle is observed to be 7.0 m/s2 and that of the second to be 9.0 m/s2. If the mass of the first particle is 5.9Ã—10-7 kg:

    1. What is the mass of the second particle?
    2. What is the magnitude of the charge of each particle?


    • Solution- 1
    Using the equation \( F = ma \), we can find the force acting on the particle;
    Given:
    \( a_1 = 7.0 \, \text{m/s}^2 \),
    \( m_1 = 5.9 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{kg} \),
    we can calculate \( F \).
    \( F = m_1 \cdot a_1 \)
    \( F = (5.9 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{kg}) \cdot (7.0 \, \text{m/s}^2) \)
    \( F \approx 4.13 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{N} \)


    Since the particles are equally charged, the force on the second particle is also \( 4.13 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{N} \); means F1=F2



    Now, let's find the mass of the second particle (\( m_2 \)) using its acceleration (\( a_2 = 9.0 \, \text{m/s}^2 \)):
    \( F = m_2 \cdot a_2 \)
    \( m_2 = \frac{F}{a_2} \)
    \( m_2 = \frac{4.13 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{N}}{9.0 \, \text{m/s}^2} \)
    \( m_2 \approx 4.59 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{kg} \)



    Solution- 2

    The electrostatic force between two charges is given by Coulomb's law:

    \( F = k \cdot \frac{q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2} \)

    Given:
    \( F = 4.13 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{N} \),
    \( k = 8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N m}^2/\text{C}^2 \),
    \( r = 5.20 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{m} \),


    we can solve for the product \( q_1 \cdot q_2 \).

    \( q_1 \cdot q_2 = \frac{F \cdot r^2}{k} \)

    \( q_1 \cdot q_2 = \frac{(4.13 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{N}) \cdot (5.20 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{m})^2}{8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N m}^2/\text{C}^2} \)

    \( q_1 \cdot q_2 \approx 1.24 \times 10^{-20} \, \text{C}^2 \)

    Since the charges are equal, \( q_1 = q_2 \), and therefore:

    \( q_1 \approx \sqrt{1.24 \times 10^{-20}} \, \text{C} \)

    \( q_1 \approx 1.114 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{C} \)

    So, the magnitude of the charge of each particle is approximately \( 1.114 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{C} \).


  • Let us consider the two protons are separated at a distance 5 nm from each other. m from each other. Compare the electrostatic force and gravitational force between them. The gravitational constant is G = \( 6.6743 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2} \) and the mass of each proton is \( 1.67262192 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg} \)



    • Let's consider two protons separated at a distance of 5 nm from each other. We want to compare the electrostatic force and gravitational force between them.

    Given data:

    • Gravitational constant (\( G \)): \( 6.6743 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2} \)
    • Mass of each proton (\( m \)): \( 1.67262192 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg} \)
    • Distance (\( r \)): 5 nm (\( 5 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m} \))


    1. Electrostatic Force:

    The electrostatic force between two protons is given by Coulomb's law:

    \( F_{\text{elec}} = k \cdot \frac{q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2} \)

    Since both protons have the same charge (\( q_1 = q_2 \)), we can rewrite the formula as:

    \( F_{\text{elec}} = k \cdot \frac{q^2}{r^2} \)

    2. Gravitational Force:

    The gravitational force between two masses is given by Newton's law of universal gravitation:

    \( F_{\text{grav}} = G \cdot \frac{m_1 \cdot m_2}{r^2} \)

    Now, let's calculate the forces:

    Electrostatic Force:

    \( F_{\text{elec}} = (8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot \frac{(1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C})^2}{(5 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m})^2} \)

    Gravitational Force:

    \( F_{\text{grav}} = (6.6743 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}) \cdot \frac{(1.67262192 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg})^2}{(5 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m})^2} \)

    Now, let's compare the values by dividing the electrostatic force by the gravitational force:

    \( \frac{F_{\text{elec}}}{F_{\text{grav}}} = \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot \frac{(1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C})^2}{(5 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m})^2}}{(6.6743 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}) \cdot \frac{(1.67262192 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg})^2}{(5 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m})^2}} \)


    \( F_{\text{elec}} = 1.2372 \times 10^{36} \cdot F_{\text{grav}} \)

    Where \( F_{\text{elec}} \) is the electrostatic force and \( F_{\text{grav}} \) is the gravitational force.


  • From the figure,


    • a. Calculate the magnitude of the net force on test charge \( q_0 \).
    b. Calculate the direction of the net force on test charge \( q_0 \).




    Given data:

    • Charge \( q_1 = 3.2 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C} \), \( r_1 = 0.005 \, \text{m} \),
    • Charge \( q_2 = 5.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C} \), \( r_2 = 0.005 \, \text{m} \),
    • Test charge \( q_0 = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C} \) .


    a. Calculating the magnitude of the net force on test charge \( q_0 \):

    \( F_{\text{q1}} = k \cdot \frac{q_1 \cdot q_0}{r_1^2} \)

    \( F_{\text{q1}} = \frac{10^{10} \cdot 3.2 \times 10^{-19} \cdot 1.6 \times 10^{-19}}{(0.005)^2} \)

    \( F_{\text{q1}} = 2.048 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{N} \)

    \( F_{\text{q2}} = k \cdot \frac{q_2 \cdot q_0}{r_2^2} \)

    \( F_{\text{q2}} = \frac{10^{10} \cdot 5.6 \times 10^{-19} \cdot 1.6 \times 10^{-19}}{(0.005)^2} \)

    \( F_{\text{q2}} = 3.548 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{N} \)

    \( F_{\text{net}} = \sqrt{F_{\text{q1}}^2 + F_{\text{q2}}^2 + 2F_{\text{q1}}F_{\text{q2}}\cos(120^\circ)} \)

    \( F_{\text{net}} = \sqrt{(2.048 \times 10^{-23})^2 + (3.548 \times 10^{-23})^2 + 2 \times 2.048 \times 10^{-23} \times 3.548 \times 10^{-23} \times \cos(120^\circ)} \)

    \( F_{\text{net}} = 3.114 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{N} \)

    b. Calculating the direction of the net force on test charge \( q_0 \):

    \( \tan(\theta) = \frac{F_{\text{q2}} \cdot \sin(120^\circ)}{F_{\text{q1}} + F_{\text{q2}} \cdot \cos(120^\circ)} \)

    \( \theta = \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{3.548 \times 10^{-23} \cdot \sin(120^\circ)}{2.048 \times 10^{-23} + 3.548 \times 10^{-23} \cdot \cos(120^\circ)} \right) \)

    \( \theta = 85.28^\circ \)

  • What is the magnitude of the electrostatic force between a singly charged sodium ion (\(Na^+\), of charge +e) and an adjacent singly charged chlorine ion (\(Cl^-\), of charge -e) in a salt crystal if their separation is \(3.81 \times 10^{-7}\) m?


    • Given:

    • Charge of \(Na^+\): \(q_1 = +e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\)
    • Charge of \(Cl^-\): \(q_2 = -e = -1.6 \times 10^{-19}\)
    • Separation distance: \(r = 3.81 \times 10^{-7}\) m


    Using Coulomb's law:

    \( F = k \cdot \frac{q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2} \)

    Substitute values:

    \( F = (8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N m}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot \frac{(|+e|) \cdot (|-e|)}{(3.81 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{m})^2} \)

    \( F = 1.58 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{N} \)

  • Point charge of + 8.0 Î¼C and - 5.0 Î¼C are placed on an x axis, at x = 8.0 m and x= 14 m. What charge must be placed at x = 26.0 m so that any test charge at x = 0 experience no electrostatic force?



    • To ensure that any test charge placed at \(x = 0\) experiences no net electrostatic force, we need to arrange the charges in such a way that the total electric field at \(x = 0\) is zero. This means that the sum of the electric fields produced by each individual charge at \(x = 0\) should add up to zero.

    The formula for the electric field produced by a point charge \(q\) at a distance \(r\) from the charge is given by:
    \[ E = \frac{k \cdot q}{r^2} \]

    Where:

    • \(E\) is the electric field
    • \(k\) is Coulomb's constant (\(8.9875 \times 10^9 \, \text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2\))
    • \(q\) is the charge
    • \(r\) is the distance from the charge


    Given that there are two charges: \(q_1 = +8.0 \, \mu\text{C}\) at \(x = 8.0 \, \text{m}\) and \(q_2 = -5.0 \, \mu\text{C}\) at \(x = 14.0 \, \text{m}\), we want to find the value of \(q_3\) at \(x = 26.0 \, \text{m}\) such that the net electric field at \(x = 0\) is zero.

    Let's denote \(r_1\) as the distance from \(q_1\) to \(x = 0\), and \(r_2\) as the distance from \(q_2\) to \(x = 0\). The distances are:
    \(r_1 = 8.0 \, \text{m}\)
    \(r_2 = 14.0 \, \text{m}\)

    The net electric field at \(x = 0\) is given by:
    \[ E_{\text{net}} = E_1 + E_2 + E_3 \]

    Since we want \(E_{\text{net}}\) to be zero, we can set up the equation:
    \[ E_1 + E_2 + E_3 = 0 \]

    Substituting the formula for electric field into the equation:
    \[ \frac{k \cdot q_1}{r_1^2} + \frac{k \cdot q_2}{r_2^2} + \frac{k \cdot q_3}{r_3^2} = 0 \]

    Solving for \(q_3\):
    \[ q_3 = -\frac{q_1 \cdot r_3^2}{r_1^2} - \frac{q_2 \cdot r_3^2}{r_2^2} \]

    Substituting the known values:
    \[ q_3 = -\frac{(+8.0 \, \mu\text{C}) \cdot (26.0 \, \text{m})^2}{(8.0 \, \text{m})^2} - \frac{(-5.0 \, \mu\text{C}) \cdot (26.0 \, \text{m})^2}{(14.0 \, \text{m})^2} \]

    Calculating the values:
    The value of \( q_3 \) is: \( -6.726 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{C} \)
